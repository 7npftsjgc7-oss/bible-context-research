# Source policy

## Preferred evidence

Use the strongest available source for each claim:

1. Biblical text in the user’s requested translation; consult original-language editions and textual apparatuses when relevant.
2. Ancient primary sources, inscriptions, papyri, archaeology reports, museum collections, and critical editions.
3. Peer-reviewed research, academic commentaries, Bible dictionaries, atlases, and monographs from established academic presses.
4. University, seminary, library, scholarly-society, museum, and reputable publisher resources.
5. General reference works only for orientation or uncontroversial summaries.

Do not treat sermons, apologetics sites, denominational advocacy, social media, Reddit, Facebook, retail listings, search snippets, or anonymous summaries as historical authorities. A confessional scholar may be used for documented scholarship, but identify the nature of the source when its commitments affect framing.

## Citation and evidence rules

- Prefer sources closest to the claim and cite the specific page or stable web page when possible.
- Use 3–8 sources for an ordinary response; use more only for a complex or disputed question.
- Do not cite a source that was not inspected.
- Separate what the biblical text states from what archaeology or later tradition attests.
- Treat absence of evidence cautiously; it is not automatically evidence of absence.
- Flag anachronisms, circular reasoning, harmonization, and maximalist/minimalist assumptions when relevant.
- For ancient sources, state date, provenance, genre, and the limit of comparison. Similarity does not establish direct dependence.
- If reliable evidence is thin, say so and narrow the claim.

## Language and textual criticism

- Use lexicons and corpus usage, not English glosses alone.
- Do not derive a word’s meaning from its etymology or root without contextual evidence.
- Distinguish lexical range from the sense used in the passage.
- Mention manuscript variants only when they materially affect wording, dating, literary analysis, or historical reconstruction.
